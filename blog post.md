<h1>Blog Post</h1> <br>

When I first entered the Applied Data Science program at Syracuse University, I had a fairly clear idea of what I thought I was signing up for. I expected to learn how to code, build models, and work with data in a structured way. I assumed the program would focus heavily on technical skills like Python, R, and machine learning algorithms, and that by the end, I would be able to analyze datasets and produce meaningful insights. <br>

While those expectations were correct, they only scratched the surface of what I actually learned. The program pushed me beyond just technical ability and helped me develop a much deeper understanding of how data is used to solve real-world problems. It also taught me how to think critically about data, communicate insights effectively, and work through ambiguity—skills that are just as important as coding, honestly to the point where it was incredibly scary to me. <br>

Coming into the program, I expected to learn three main things: technical skills, statistical methods, and data visualization. I wanted to become comfortable programming in R and Python, working with datasets, and building models. I expected to learn about regression, hypothesis testing, and basic machine learning techniques. I also thought I would learn how to present results using charts, dashboards, and other visual tools. <br>

When I look back, I did gain a deeper learning of all of these things, but what surprised me most was how interconnected they are. I initially viewed data science as a set of separate skills. There is coding, modeling, and visualization, but the program showed me that these are all part of a larger process that brings insight to all aspects of life. Success in data science is not just about building a model, it is about understanding the problem, preparing the data, selecting the right approach, and communicating the results in a meaningful way. <br>

One of the biggest takeaways from both the undergraduate and graduate programs is that real-world data is rarely clean or ready to use. While it can suck at times, most of the real world data requires preparing the data so that models can even be used. This was something I had heard before, but I did not fully understand it until I experienced it myself. <br>

I encountered this most clearly in my Dog Adoption Analysis project, where I worked with over 350,000 records from multiple animal shelters across different cities. Each dataset had its own structure, naming conventions, and level of completeness. Variables such as breed, color, outcome type, and sex were labeled differently depending on which city the source came from and there were many missing or inconsistent values that I had to clean up. <br>

Before I could begin any meaningful analysis, I had to spend a significant amount of time cleaning and standardizing the data. This involved merging datasets with different schemas, reconciling inconsistent labels, and creating new variables that could be used across all the different sources. For example, in my dog adoption project I had to group similar breed names together, standardize outcome categories, and create derived variables such as age and age group. <br>

This process was both challenging and eye-opening. It required patience, attention to detail, and a willingness to iterate. I often found that fixing one issue would reveal another, forcing me to revisit earlier steps and refine my approach. Through this experience, I learned how to handle missing data, identify inconsistencies, and make decisions about how to structure the dataset in a way that supports analysis. <br>

Another important aspect of this project was feature engineering. This involves creating variables such as age groups which allowed me to better understand patterns in the data and improve the performance of my models. It also showed me that the way data is represented can have a significant impact on the insights you are able to extract. When I was conducting my analysis, I tested with both the cleaned and uncleaned data and before I cleaned the data, it was messy and the results were difficult to interpret. This further proved my previous point. <br>

Beyond the technical skills, my dog adoption project also taught me how to think more critically about data. I had to consider questions such as... <br>
1. What does this variable actually represent? 
2. Is this data reliable? Are there biases or inconsistencies that could affect the results? <br>

These are not questions that can be answered with code alone—they require judgment and an understanding of the context in which the data was collected.

Once the data was cleaned and prepared, I was able to move into the analysis phase. Using statistical methods and regression modeling, I evaluated which variables had the strongest impact on adoption outcomes. The results showed that factors such as sex and sterilization status were highly influential, while age also played a significant role. Other variables, such as color and breed, were less consistent in their effects. <br>

What stood out to me was how these findings challenged some common assumptions. For example, there is often a belief that certain breeds are more likely to be adopted than others, but the data suggested that more practical factors, such as age and whether an animal was spayed or neutered, had a much stronger impact. This reinforced the idea that data can provide a more objective perspective and help move beyond intuition or anecdotal evidence. <br>

Another key lesson from this project was the importance of communication. After completing the analysis, I needed to present my findings in a way that was clear and meaningful. This involved creating visualizations such as distribution plots and boxplots to highlight trends and relationships in the data. It also required me to explain my results in plain language, focusing on the key takeaways rather than the technical details. <br>

This aspect of the project helped me realize that being able to communicate insights is just as important as being able to generate them. A well-built model is only valuable if others can understand and act on the results. Learning how to tell a story with data—using both visuals and narrative—was a critical part of my development in the program. <br>

Overall, the dog ddoption analysis project (project 6) was one of the most impactful experiences in my time in the program. It brought together many of the skills I had been developing, including data cleaning, feature engineering, statistical modeling, and visualization. More importantly, it changed the way I think about data science. I no longer see it as just a technical discipline, but as a process that involves problem-solving, critical thinking, and communication. <br>

This experience also gave me a better appreciation for the complexity of real-world data. It showed me that there is rarely a straightforward path from raw data to final insights, and that the process often involves trial and error, iteration, and refinement. While this can be challenging, it is also what makes data science interesting and rewarding. <br>

In the end, what I learned from this project, and from the program as a whole, is that the most valuable skill in data science is not just knowing how to use tools or build models, but knowing how to approach a problem, work through uncertainty, and extract meaningful insights from imperfect data. <br>

When it comes to things I learned over the course of my time at Syracuse I have learned 4 things:

1. Data is Messy and Taking The Time To Clean it is Important

One of the biggest takeaways from the program is that real-world data is rarely clean or ready to use. Before starting the program, I assumed that most of the work in data science would revolve around building models and writing code. However, I quickly learned that the majority of time is actually spent preparing data so that meaningful analysis can even take place. Data cleaning is not just a preliminary step, it is genuinely the foundation of the entire process. <br>

I experienced this most clearly, again, in my Dog Adoption Analysis project, where I worked with over 350,000 records from multiple animal shelters across different cities. Each dataset came with its own structure, naming conventions, and inconsistencies. Variables such as breed, color, and outcome type were labeled differently across sources, and there were significant amounts of missing or incomplete data. <br>

Before I could begin analyzing the data, I had to standardize these variables and ensure consistency across all datasets. This involved merging datasets with different schemas, resolving discrepancies in naming, and deciding how to handle missing values in a way that would not bias the results. For example, I had to group similar breed names together, standardize outcome categories, and create consistent labels for variables like sex and sterilization status. <br>

Another key part of this process was feature engineering. I created new variables, such as age and age groups, which allowed for more meaningful analysis and helped uncover patterns that were not immediately visible in the raw data. This step reinforced the idea that the way data is structured and represented has a direct impact on the insights that can be derived from it. <br>

Through this project, I learned how to merge datasets with different structures, handle missing and inconsistent data, and engineer features to improve analysis. More importantly, I learned that data cleaning is not just about fixing errors—it requires critical thinking and decision-making. Every choice made during this process can influence the final results, which makes it one of the most important and challenging aspects of data science. <br>

2. Models Are Only as Good as the Problem You’re Solving <br>

Another major learning outcome was understanding that building a model is not the be all end all of a problem set. The real goal is creating a solution to a meaningful problem. Early on, I focused heavily on model performance, thinking that a higher accuracy, or R-squared, automatically meant a better result. Over time, I realized that a model is only valuable if it provides insights that can be used to make decisions. <br>

This became clear in my MLB Attendance Analysis project, where I analyzed data from over 23,000 games to understand what drives fan attendance. I built multiple regression models and refined them using a stepwise approach, ultimately developing a model that explained approximately 73% of the variance in attendance. <br>

While achieving strong model performance was important, the most valuable part of the project was the insights that came from it. The analysis showed that factors such as star players and weekend scheduling significantly increase attendance, while weather conditions and venue characteristics also play a major role. At the same time, some expected relationships—such as late-season divisional games having a strong impact—were weaker than anticipated. <br>

The MLB project really told me that... <br>

* A simpler model with clear, interpretable insights is often more valuable than a highly complex one <br>
* The goal of analysis is to inform decisions, not just maximize accuracy <br>
* Interpretation and storytelling are just as important as technical performance <br>

It also reinforced the importance of aligning the model with the problem being solved. In this case, the objective was not just to predict attendance, but to understand what drives it in a way that teams could use for marketing and engagement strategies. This shift in perspective changed how I approach modeling and analysis. <br>

3. Data Can Answer Real-World Questions in Surprising Ways <br>

One of the most interesting aspects of the program was seeing how data can challenge assumptions and reveal patterns that are not immediately obvious. Many of the projects I worked on started with a question that seemed straightforward, but the results often told a more complex story. <br>

In my Fast Food Health Analysis project, I explored the question, which fast food restaurant is actually the healthiest? To answer this, I created a custom “health score” based on calories, fat, and sodium, which allowed me to compare menu items across major chains in a consistent way. <br>

The results were surprising. Restaurants that are commonly perceived as “healthier” did not always perform better when evaluated using the data. Instead, there was significant variation within each restaurant’s menu, and factors such as portion size played a major role in determining overall health impact. In many cases, the difference between items within a single restaurant was greater than the difference between restaurants. <br>

The fast food project really showed me... <br>

* Data can challenge widely held beliefs and assumptions <br>
* The way a concept is defined and measured (in this case, “health”) can significantly influence the results <br>
* Visualization is a powerful tool for uncovering and communicating patterns <br>

It also highlighted the importance of framing the problem correctly. By creating a custom metric, I was able to approach the question in a structured way and generate insights that would not have been possible using raw variables alone. <br>

4. Combining Methods Strengthens Analysis <br>

As I progressed through the program, I also learned the value of using multiple analytical approaches to strengthen and validate results. Rather than relying on a single method, combining different techniques can provide a more complete understanding of the data. <br>

In my Dog Movie Popularity project, I replicated an academic study analyzing how movies influence dog breed popularity. To evaluate the data, I applied both frequentist and Bayesian methods, including hypothesis testing, regression analysis, and Bayesian inference. <br>

Using both approaches allowed me to compare results across different statistical frameworks and confirm that the findings were consistent. This not only increased my confidence in the results but also provided additional insight into how different methods interpret the same data. <br>

During my time working on the replication study, I learned that... <br>

* There is rarely one correct method for analyzing data <br>
* Different approaches can provide complementary perspectives <br>
* Strong analysis involves validating results from multiple angles <br>

This project also deepened my understanding of statistical reasoning and reinforced the importance of questioning assumptions and verifying results. <br>

When I look back on my time in the Applied Data Science program at Syracuse University, I can confidently say that I gained far more than I initially expected. While I entered the program focused on learning technical skills like coding and modeling, I am leaving with a much deeper understanding of how data science is applied in the real world. The program taught me that success in this field is not defined by how complex your model is, but by how effectively you can use data to solve meaningful problems.

Across my projects, I developed the ability to work with messy, real-world data, think critically about the decisions I make during analysis, and communicate insights in a way that others can understand. Whether it was cleaning and standardizing hundreds of thousands of records in my dog adoption project, building predictive models to understand fan behavior in my MLB attendance analysis, or creating custom metrics to challenge assumptions in my fast food study, each experience contributed to my growth as both a student and a problem solver.

One of the most important lessons I learned is that data science is an iterative and often imperfect process. There is rarely a clear starting point or a single correct solution. Instead, it requires patience, adaptability, and the willingness to continuously refine your approach. This was one of the biggest surprises for me, but it is also what makes the field so engaging. Every dataset presents a new challenge, and every project requires a different way of thinking.

Another key takeaway is the importance of communication. Being able to build a model is valuable, but being able to explain what that model means and why it matters is just as important. Throughout the program, I learned how to translate technical results into clear, actionable insights, which is a skill I will carry forward in my career.

Overall, the program has given me a strong foundation in both the technical and practical aspects of data science. More importantly, it has changed the way I approach problems. I now think more critically, ask better questions, and rely on data to guide decision-making rather than assumptions. As I move forward, I feel confident in my ability to apply these skills to real-world challenges and continue growing in the field of data science.
