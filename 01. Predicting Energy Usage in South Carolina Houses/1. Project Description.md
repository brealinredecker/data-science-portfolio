<h1>Project Description</h1><br>

This project focused on predicting residential electricity usage in South Carolina during peak summer conditions to help an energy provider anticipate strain on the electrical grid. Using a dataset of over 4,600 observations and 228 variables, including building characteristics, appliance usage, and weather data. I developed and evaluated multiple regression models to identify the key drivers of energy consumption.<br>

The analysis was structured around three modeling approaches: building infrastructure, appliance usage, and weather variables. Initial models revealed that appliance usage was the strongest standalone predictor, explaining approximately 86% of the variance in electricity usage, while weather variables alone contributed very little predictive power.<br>

I then developed a refined comprehensive model by combining the most significant variables across all categories. This model achieved strong performance, explaining over 90% of the variance and demonstrating a close fit between predicted and actual values. Key drivers included HVAC setpoints, appliance usage (particularly cooling systems and plug loads), and building characteristics such as floor area and system type. Weather variables, while statistically significant in some cases, had a much smaller overall impact than expected.<br>

Using the final model, I estimated peak electricity demand for July and identified patterns that could contribute to grid overload during extreme heat conditions. These insights highlight the importance of managing cooling behavior, improving appliance efficiency, and planning for peak demand periods.<br>

Overall, this project demonstrates my ability to work with high-dimensional data, build and refine predictive models, and translate technical findings into actionable insights for real-world decision-making.<br>

<h2>Key Results</h2>
- Comprehensive model explained approximately 90% of the variance in electricity usage <br>
- Appliance usage and HVAC settings were the strongest predictors <br>
- Weather variables had minimal standalone predictive impact <br>
